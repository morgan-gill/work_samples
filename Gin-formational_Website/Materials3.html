This page goes over some of the ingredients and equipment used in the production process of Gin. Several common ingredients have been listed as "optional," as they are not absolutely necessary. Never the less, many brewers add them to the spirit as they believe that they enhance the flavour of the finished product.

Distilled Water:

Clean distilled water will provide a good medium for the barley grains to mix and release their sugars. This will provide an ideal environment for the yeast to consume the sugars, produce alcohol and multiply.

Barley:

Barley is a type of grass, similar to wheat. The grains that it produces are a common ingredient in many types of alcoholic beverages.

Yeast:

Yeast is a type of fungus that is vital to the brewing process. Yeast will consume carbohydrates (complex sugars) and produce a mix of both carbon dioxide and alcohol. The alcohol that the yeast produces is a special type of alcohol known as ethanol.

Fermenter:

A fermenter or fermentation tank is a large vessel that is used to hold your mix of grains, yeast, and water. This mixture is commonly referred to as the "Mash."

Distiller:

One of the most vital pieces of equipment in the process. The distiller is used to separate the ethanol from the mash. The distiller it's self is made up of multiple components which we'll go over in more detail on the "Production" page.

Sieve:

The sieve is used to hold your flavouring ingredients during the distillation process, preventing them from becoming part of the mash but still allowing their flavours to influence the mixture.

Juniper Berries:

Junipers are coniferous plants that are capable of growing in a wide variety of places on earth. They are mostly scattered through the northern hemisphere. Juniper berries are what give Gin it's iconic flavour. As such, it must be the primary flavouring ingredient during production.
